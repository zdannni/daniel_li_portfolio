01 · Overview

A prototype without a proven audience.

Tripal was a travel-planning app built by a small startup team. They had a working prototype and a founder's personal conviction. What they did not have was a validated understanding of who the product was for, what moments it addressed, or why someone would adopt it over existing tools. A business competition jury had already torn those assumptions apart in front of them.

Over two months I designed and led a research program that answered the jury's questions, reshaped the feature set, and fed directly into an investor pitch. The team placed second at the competition and secured early investor funding. This case study is about owning a research cycle end to end when both the product question and the timeline are unforgiving.

02 · The Research Challenge

A founder's frustration is not yet a product thesis.

The team's idea came from a real experience. Planning a winter trip to Iceland with five friends across six countries had turned into a logistical nightmare of conflicting dates, buried recommendations, and budget disputes. The frustration was the spark, and the prototype was the first response.

When the team took the idea to a business startup competition, the judges' feedback was pointed and specific.

Competition feedback · Before research

What the judges said about the pitch.

I question the choice to start with Chinese students. This is a relatively small demographic that probably has less expendable income and travels less frequently than other demographics.

Judge A · on target market

Viral growth is the most important thing for this kind of app. It's a service where you invite the people you're travelling with. This needs to be addressed.

Judge B · on growth strategy

You need to tell a better, more refined story around target market, monetization, and differentiation.

Judge C · on positioning

Love the idea. Needs more user testing. Curious why students are your first market group when recent grads and young professionals generally have more disposable income?

Judge D · on research depth

Four judges, four concrete gaps. Target market, growth, monetization, and whether the team had actually talked to users. That is where the research came in.

I joined the team with a specific job. Answer the judges' questions with real evidence, validate or redirect the product direction, and produce enough clarity to justify a feature set in time for the investor pitch two weeks after the research concluded. The founder's Iceland story was real. It was also one person's experience. The research needed to tell us which parts of that frustration generalized, to whom, and in what specific moments.

03 · My Approach

A sequence designed for convergence.

I designed the program as a deliberate sequence. Qualitative grounding first, quantitative validation second, synchronous team convergence third. Each phase earned the next.

Research program structure · 2 months
Phase 01

Desktop research

Week 1
  • Industry reports (Euromonitor)
  • Academic research
  • Competitive landscape
  • Reddit qual sampling
Phase 02

Interviews

Weeks 2 to 4
  • 12 in-depth interviews
  • Mixed solo & group travelers
  • Diverse trip types
  • Saturation reached
Phase 03

Survey

Weeks 5 to 6
  • 330 responses
  • Qual patterns tested at scale
  • Pain point prioritization
  • Attitudes toward AI in planning
Phase 04

Design sprint

Weeks 7 to 8
  • Two 3-hour workshops
  • Full team absorbed research
  • Converged on feature set
  • I facilitated both

Key methodological choices I made

Sequencing

Qualitative before quantitative, always.

Running the survey before interviews would have meant guessing at what to measure. I used the interviews to surface what mattered, then used the survey to test whether those patterns held at scale. By the time I wrote the survey questions, the data had already told me which ones were worth asking.

Sample sizing

12 interviews was a saturation judgment.

Twelve is not a magic number. It was the point at which I was hearing repeated themes rather than new ones, across a sample I had deliberately diversified by travel style, group size, and trip frequency. I treated saturation as a judgment call I could defend, not a rule I was following.

Format

Design sprint instead of a research report.

A report handed off to a distracted team two weeks before a pitch would not have produced alignment in time. The sprint forced synchronous absorption, collective ideation against the findings, and convergence on a feature set in the same room. Speed and alignment at the same time, which regular meetings cannot give you both of.

Facilitation

Two sessions, not one.

I split the sprint across two three-hour sessions because ideation benefits from a night of subconscious processing. Day one was research immersion and divergent ideation. Day two was critique and convergence. The split is what turned "lots of ideas" into "a small set we would actually build."

04 · Key Findings

Three nuggets. Two drove features. One got deferred.

Three findings held up across both the interviews and the survey. Each one is captured below as an atomic research nugget: observation, evidence, and tags for severity, theme, and phase of the travel journey.

01
Observation

Travel planning is not one decision. It is a spiral of micro-decisions that produces choice paralysis.

Unlike structured decision-making models, travel planning is a multi-layered, iterative process that forces travelers to navigate across multiple platforms and dozens of interdependent decisions. Destination, dates, flights, accommodations, activities, coordination with others, budget. The spiral produces choice paralysis and decision fatigue, and it reframes what the product needs to do.

Tripal did not need to become a better destination database. It needed to be a tool that reduced cognitive switching cost.

Evidence
I check flights, then I go check hotel prices, then I have to remember that number. Then I switch to figure out where I want to go each day. Later, when booking a train, I realize I need to check my hotel's checkout time again, then come back and adjust the ticket. It's a constant back-and-forth.P07 · interview
27.3% of survey respondents explicitly identified "too many options to decide" as a major pain point in the travel planning process.
Planning just takes a lot of time, and you have this fear of missing out. You want to visit all the places, but at the end you're missing out because you spend too much time focusing on the plan.P03 · interview
Tags critical cognitive-load decision-fatigue planning-phase
02
Observation

Unexpected disruptions frustrate travelers more than the disruptions themselves warrant.

Even when people planned thoroughly (booking early, reading blogs, coordinating routes) they still ran into weather changes, delayed flights, restaurant closures, or sudden illness. These events did not just break the schedule. They emotionally deflated the entire experience. The pain was not the disruption itself. It was the absence of a graceful fallback.

Users wanted backup plans suggested before they needed them, not after the situation had already collapsed.

Evidence
29.4% of survey respondents selected "handling unexpected situations" as a core challenge, making it the single most-cited pain point in the dataset.
Our flight was delayed and we had to rebook the hotel. Then someone got sick, so the whole day had to change. I just slept in the car while my friend drove.P09 · interview
It'd be great to have backup plans suggested ahead of time when something goes wrong. I wish there was a system that could warn me about local risks before I ran into them.P12 · interview
Tags critical recovery adaptive-planning in-trip-phase
03
Observation

Group travel produces a specific kind of pain that solo travel does not.

Traveling with friends or family is what most people prefer, but coordinating such trips often produces tension. Diverging preferences, poor communication, and unequal planning labor all compound. People described the emotional cost of being the planner: the social risk of feeling too controlling, the frustration of unresponsive group chats, and the quiet resentment of carrying the load alone.

Several interviewees told me they deliberately limited their travel companions to three or fewer specifically to avoid this pain. The insight pointed at group coordination as a distinct problem space, not a flavor of the planning spiral.

Evidence
26.1% of survey respondents flagged "coordinating group schedules" as one of their top pain points.
My family usually has one person who ends up doing all the real planning. Others might help a bit, but not really. When I make plans, I always worry they'll think I'm being too controlling.P05 · interview
Three people are the upper limit for me. Beyond that, it's just too hard to coordinate. I only travel with close friends or family. If the group gets too big, my own plans always get compromised.P11 · interview
Tags deferred social-dynamics coordination-labor cross-phase
!

The hardest call the research enabled.

The first two insights drove every core feature the team decided to build. The third, group coordination, was the founder's original motivation for the entire product. And it was the one we pushed into a future-work column rather than v1.

Building group features first felt emotionally right. But the research said that solving cognitive switching cost and disruption recovery would reach a broader audience, and fit within the build timeline we had. Letting the research override the founder's origin story was the right decision. It was only possible because the research had produced findings the team trusted enough to act against their first instinct.

05 · Impact & Decisions Influenced

Four features, one deferral, and a pitch that landed.

The design sprint converged the team on four core features. Each one maps directly back to an insight the research produced, and each one represents a decision the team would not have made the same way without the data under it.

◆ from nugget 01

Smart Link & Photo Recognition

Import travel inspiration from social media in one tap. The app detects restaurants, landmarks, and locations from shared links or screenshots, and saves them to the itinerary automatically. Reduces the mental overhead of toggling between apps.

◆ from nugget 01

Smart Itinerary Planning

Flexible, well-paced multi-day itineraries with AI assistance and manual control. Activity-level planning, transit times built in, and a structure that adapts to travel style. Cuts the planning effort that was producing choice paralysis.

◆ from nugget 01

Map View

Turn the trip into a spatial object rather than a list. Pin locations by day, preview routes, and understand at a glance whether the plan makes geographic sense. Reduces backtracking and improves schedule efficiency.

◆ from nugget 02

Adaptive Suggestion

Detect disruptions like weather, closures, or transit delays, and proactively suggest alternatives before the plan collapses. Directly answers the most-cited pain point in the survey data, and reframes the app as a trip safety net.

Round of usability testing

Pre-launch iterations on labels, icons, and mobile sizing.

After the team converged on features, I ran a round of usability testing with four participants simulating a two-day Paris trip. The test surfaced concrete iterations rather than headline findings. Labels like "Travel Insights" and "Batch import" were renamed, the AI "magic button" iconography was clarified, attraction cards were enlarged for mobile, and the weather alert was redesigned to be more proportionate. These are the kind of pre-pitch corrections that make the difference between a demo that lands and a demo that confuses.

Pitch outcome · Fall 2024

The research held its weight in front of real investors.

Target market became grounded in qual and quant data. Differentiation became tied to specific user moments that existing tools did not handle. The judges' questions from two months earlier each had an evidence-backed answer.

2nd place
At the business startup competition. The same judges who had torn the original pitch apart recognized the new one.
Early funding
Secured from investors after the pitch. The research was not the only thing that mattered, but the pitch would not have landed the same way without it.
06 · Reflection

Lessons I carry forward.

Tripal was the first time I owned a research cycle end to end under real time pressure, in front of real stakeholders, with real stakes attached. Two lessons stuck, and I carry both forward into how I think about UXR now.

The first is about timeline discipline. The research took most of the project window, and the design sprint took most of what was left, which meant we had almost no room for iteration after the feature set was chosen. If I ran this again, I would compress the early desktop and interview phases to buy back a week of iteration time at the end. The value of iteration in a startup context is hard to overstate. The cost of being wrong about a feature compounds with every day you cannot revise it.

The second lesson is more specific and has changed how I think about synthesis. In the design sprint, I presented the interview data as organized clusters, arranged into a framework that mapped to the stages of travel decision-making. The framework was sound. The participants in the sprint could not fully digest the volume of data even with that structure. They absorbed the framework shape but not the texture of what people actually said, which is where a lot of the product intuition should have come from.

If I ran this again, I would try a different artifact for the sprint. Possibly feeding the interview data into a tool like NotebookLM to create a conversational interface the team could query directly. Or building a smaller set of synthetic user personas grounded in the interview material that team members could interrogate during ideation. The principle I take from this is that research artifacts are not just containers for findings. They are interfaces between researchers and the people who have to act on the findings, and that interface has its own usability to design for.

On timelines

Next time, compress the front end to buy back iteration time. A week of iteration is worth three days of extra fieldwork.

On artifacts

Research artifacts are interfaces. The sprint deck is a product, and sprint participants are users. Design for that.

On trade-offs

The hardest research moments are when findings push back against the founder's original instinct. Respecting that tension is part of the job.
