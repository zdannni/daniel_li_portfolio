About me

Research is a craft. I treat it that way.

I'm Daniel, a UX Researcher and Research Operations practitioner based in Los Angeles. I help product teams turn messy, ambiguous questions into research that stakeholders trust enough to act on.

My path into research came through caring about people and being allergic to research that sits unread. I studied Cognitive Science at UC San Diego and earned a Master's in Information from the University of Michigan, where I learned that methods are the easy part — it's the judgment underneath them that makes research useful.

A short biography.

I came to user research through a winding path. I studied Cognitive Science at UC San Diego because I was interested in how people make decisions under uncertainty, and I continued at the University of Michigan where I specialized in UX research and research methods. Along the way, I learned that the most interesting research problems live at the edges — where a team's assumptions meet the messy reality of how people actually think, and where what a product claims to offer meets what users actually experience.

My Safemo internship taught me what research operations actually means. It's not administration — it's the sum of small, deliberate choices about how research gets framed, who it speaks to, and how it lands with the people who have to act on it. I proposed the rainbow-spreadsheet synthesis approach, the product-market fit versus usability framing, and the chronological presentation that mirrored how users actually experienced the setup process. None of those were spelled out in the research plan; they were judgment calls made in service of the team making better decisions.

My Tripal capstone taught me what it means to own a full research cycle under real time pressure — scoping, method selection, interviewing, synthesizing, facilitating a design sprint, and watching the research hold its weight in front of real investors. The research placed second at a business competition and helped the team secure early funding. That outcome mattered to me less than what the project taught me about translation: research is only as useful as the translation between what you find and what the team can act on.

I write, speak, and work in both English and Mandarin, which means I can bridge research practice between US and Chinese product teams — something I care about increasingly as more of the interesting product work happens across that bridge.

How I work

Four principles I carry into every project.

01 / Scoping

The framing decision is the research decision.

A research question phrased one way is tractable; phrased another way it's impossible. The most consequential choice I make on any project happens before I open a doc — it's deciding which version of the question the research is actually going to answer.

02 / Methods

Methods are chosen for reasons, and I can explain them.

Mixed-methods work gets muddled when the sequencing isn't intentional. I sequence interviews before surveys because qualitative work surfaces what to measure, and I choose tools like diary studies or design sprints when the constraint of the project makes them the right answer, not because they're impressive.

03 / Synthesis

Good synthesis compresses rather than accumulates.

A research report with forty findings is a report no one acts on. My job in synthesis is to find the two or three insights that actually shift decisions, frame them so they land with the team, and push the rest into an appendix without apologizing.

04 / Communication

How a finding lands is part of the research.

Research that stakeholders can't act on is research that hasn't done its job. I design presentations the same way I design studies — with a theory about how people will encounter the findings and what they need in order to make decisions from them.
