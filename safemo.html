01 · Overview

A privacy-first product that users couldn't use.

Safemo's wire-free security camera launched with a privacy-first promise — local storage, on-device AI, no cloud, no monthly fees — and missed its sales projections. Amazon reviews were brutal. The product had been engineered around a real value proposition, but something between the box and the user's experience was breaking.

Over two rounds of longitudinal mixed-methods research, the team cut setup time from an hour and a half to under twenty minutes and lifted NPS from 3 to 8.5. This is the story of how the research framing and analytical choices I pushed for helped turn early warnings into decisions stakeholders acted on.

02 · The Research Challenge

The problems were visible, but not legible.

By the time the research started, the product's problems were visible but not legible. Sales had disappointed, reviews were negative, and the team knew something was wrong — but "wrong" was spread across hardware setup, a confusing app, AI features that weren't landing, and a privacy story that was central to the marketing but unclear in the product.

The senior researcher's mandate was to untangle this into two questions: where was product-market fit actually breaking, and where were the usability failures costing users before they ever got to experience the features?

A user who cannot finish setup never experiences the privacy feature — you cannot tell if the value proposition is weak or if users simply never got to perceive it.

That framing sounds clean in retrospect. The harder part was that these two questions are deeply tangled in a real product. We needed a research design that could separate these layers — and a synthesis approach that would not collapse them back together.

03 · My Approach

Two rounds, layered methods, deliberate sequencing.

The study was designed by a senior researcher as a two-round longitudinal program. Round two would repeat the structure of round one after the team implemented changes, so we could test whether our framing was right.

My first contribution was protocol design. I drafted the participant screener, the usability test moderation guide, the diary study prompts, and the follow-up interview protocol. The constraint that shaped most of those drafts was participant burden — we were asking people to host a camera system in their home for weeks, and every minute of unnecessary protocol friction would compound over the study.

Key methodological choices I made

Synthesis method

Proposed rainbow spreadsheet analysis

Five participants with multiple data streams each (setup observation, two interviews, two weeks of diary entries) required a structure that could hold both within-participant patterns and cross-participant themes without losing either.

Analytical framing

Separated PMF from usability findings

Conflating "the privacy story is weak" with "users cannot finish setup" would have made the recommendations incoherent. I proposed two parallel tracks so the team could act on each without mixing them up.

Presentation structure

Split setup from sustained daily use

A first-time setup user and a two-week power user are not the same person. Combining their feedback would have muddled the recommendations for both audiences.

Narrative ordering

Chronological mirror of user mental model

I ordered the usability findings along the user's actual journey — unboxing, setup, first use, sustained use — so stakeholders could map findings directly to their product flow.

04 · Key Insights

Three findings that did most of the work.

Out of dozens of issues identified across setup, daily use, AI features, and privacy perception, three findings shaped what happened next.

01

The setup experience was failing before users ever saw the product.

Unclear component labeling, a manual split across four separate guides with an ambiguous sequence, and a power button hidden under the battery cap combined to create a setup process that took an average of 90 minutes. One participant could not activate the camera without the moderator's help.

This was not a polish issue — it was an abandonment risk sitting at the front door of the product.

From these four things, I'm actually not sure what's the first one. It's not immediately obvious how to connect this stuff, or what it goes to.

Participant 3 · Round 1 setup test

Evidence
On average, participants spent over 3 minutes figuring out how to activate the camera. One participant could not finish activation without moderator help. Average setup time across Round 1: 1.5 hours.
02

The privacy value proposition was not landing the way the team expected.

The strategic centerpiece of the product — local storage, on-device AI — was the marketing story. But the research revealed that users either barely noticed it (because setup had not emphasized it), questioned whether an unknown company's privacy claims could be trusted, or cared less about privacy for outdoor cameras than the product's positioning assumed.

The finding was not that privacy did not matter. It was that Safemo's specific implementation of privacy did not match how users actually thought about it.

This company doesn't have any known reputation to me. They're claiming what they do and they need to have some established good track record.

Participant 4 · Round 1 interview

03

The word "extensions" made the core features feel peripheral.

This one emerged unexpectedly from the interview transcripts. Users were confused by the AI features being labeled as "extensions" in the app — four of five participants associated the word with plugins or third-party integrations, not with core functionality. What the team had intended as a technical descriptor was actively making the features feel peripheral and intimidating.

This insight was not on any interview guide. It surfaced from pattern recognition across quotes that all said some version of "I don't know what an extension is here." It was in the data, waiting to be noticed.

I don't think of these as extensions. I think of extensions as plug-ins with other vendors. These look like core functionality to me.

05 · Impact & Decisions Influenced

What changed — and what I owned in Round 2.

The round-one findings went to stakeholders with a concrete set of recommendations: rewrite the manual, add explicit power-button guidance, rename the AI "extensions," restructure app navigation, and reposition privacy messaging around specific use cases. The team adopted most of these and implemented changes to the manual and the app's information architecture.

I owned Round 2 end-to-end — recruitment, moderation, diary study coordination, data collection, analysis, and the final report. Round 2 was the test of whether Round 1's framing had been right. If the setup findings were real, setup time should drop. If the navigation problems were real, discoverability should improve. The results validated the framing.

For the final stakeholder presentation, I proposed a before-and-after slide that put Round 1 and Round 2 metrics side by side, and I chose which metrics to highlight. This was deliberate. The team had acted on our Round 1 recommendations in good faith, and I wanted the presentation to visibly credit them for doing so. Stakeholders who see their decisions validated by research are more likely to commission more research — that's as much a research operations decision as it is a communication one.

06 · Reflection

Lessons I carry forward.

Every project teaches you something about the craft. Here are the lessons I carry forward from Safemo — alongside honest callouts in the margin.

The biggest lesson from Safemo is that research operations is a craft of small, deliberate choices, not a set of administrative tasks. Every decision I made about framing, presentation structure, and which metrics to put on the final slide was shaping how the findings would land with the team. None of those choices were in the research plan. They came out of paying attention to the stakeholders as carefully as we paid attention to the users.

I also learned that validation is its own kind of contribution. Round 2 could have felt like execution work, but owning it end-to-end taught me that the second round of a longitudinal study is where you find out whether your framing was actually right. That's as close to research feedback as you get, and it changes how you design the next study.

On synthesis

The rainbow spreadsheet was the right call for this study size, but I'd pair it with a qualitative coding tool next time to preserve more of the individual voice.

On stakeholders

The before-and-after slide was the most-referenced artifact in the final meeting. Visual framing of validation matters as much as the validation itself.
